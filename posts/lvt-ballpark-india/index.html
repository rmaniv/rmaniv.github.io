Estimating how much a country’s land is worth (and the capitalization rates) is important to know how much revenue can a Land Value Tax (LVT) raise. Many have tried to arrive at estimates for the US, Canada and Australia. I haven’t seen any estimate for India. In this post, I’m going to provide an (interim; because of lack of data and the free time that I have) estimate for how much tax can be raised from land for India.

1 What to expect from this estimate

  • The aim of this post is not to arrive at a “state of the art” or the most accurate estimate for how much we can raise by levying an LVT in India but just a ballpark that can be used to inform the aims of LVT advocates and to know if it’s even worth to look at from a “revenue-raising point of view.”

  • This obviously doesn’t take into account the dynamic effects of levying an LVT, i.e. it doesn’t account the change in how much revenue an LVT can raise if land value change after the introduction of the tax.

2 Land value excluding natural resources (oil, gas, minerals, forests etc)

The land value estimates for US, Canada and Australia were published by their government–Federal Reserve Flow of Funds in the case of US, National Accounts Balance Sheets in the case of Canada and Australian Bureau of Statistics in the case of Australia. Those estimates were usually based on the property prices recorded at the time of their last sale minus the replacement cost of the structures built on the land. These are likely to be underestimates given that they don’t take the price appreciation of land into account. In addition, McKinsey, in its “The rise and rise of the global balance sheet” report provides land value estimates for 10 countries and extrapolates that for the world.

Their estimate for global land value is just around (a bit less than) 2 times GDP. For India, that would be around Rs. 552 lakh crores (1 lakh crore = 1 trillion) given that our GDP for FY23 was around Rs. 276 lakh crores.

To check if the global average fits right for India, I’ll estimate the “circle rate value of Delhi” and check if it is 2 times Delhi’s GSDP. Circle rate is the assessed value per square meter published by the government for a particular neighborhood and they’re known to be gross underestimates. There are just 8 circle rate categories for the entire city of Delhi (with an area of ~1400 square kilometers and a population of more than 25 million) and each neighborhood falls in one of those 8.

2.1 Spot check with circle rate value of Delhi

This repository contains code that I wrote to find the circle rate value of Delhi. The program looks up the addresses of all properties within the perimeter of Delhi filtered from Google’s OpenBuildings dataset by requesting a human-readable address from OpenStreetMap’s Nominatim API’s reverse geocoding address look-up feature and drops any addresses that are not in Delhi. Then, it matches the address to a circle rate category and multiples the circle rate with the land area. I was thinking of adjusting each property for maximum ground coverage requirements of the government to figure out a more accurate land area (since OpenBuildings only provides the building footprint area) but given that it’s not even able to do the address - circle rate category matching, I thought adjusting areas would just be a waste of time. The address returned by OpenStreetMap’s Nominatim API may not correspond to the ones in the circle rate category list, so it’s not possible for it to assign circle rates to each property. Ideally, the municipal government should have released Shapefiles for circle rate categories like city governments in the West do for tax assessor data, parcel data, zoning data, property sale transaction data etc. I haven’t been able to find any for Delhi. I’d not be surprised if disputes on circle rates between tax filers and the tax department in courts were being resolved using non-digitized maps.

I’ll probably file an RTI to get those maps soon and digitize them manually but that will take a lot of time. Until then, I’ll just divide 2 times of GDP with Delhi’s total area and check if that “land value per unit area” estimate sits well with Delhi’s circle rates.

Delhi’s GSDP for FY23 was about Rs. 10.4 lakh crores. 2 times this is Rs. 20.8 lakh crores.

Delhi’s “Master Plan for 2021”, that was prepared in 2007, provides the following figures for land-use distribution.

Dividing Rs. 20.8 lakh crores by 487.6 square kilometers, we get ~Rs. 42,658 per square meter.

This means that assuming land values to be 2 times Delhi’s GSDP by the area for residential, commercial and industrial land, we get a land value per unit area that is lower than the second lowest circle rate category. I think this shows that our estimate is reasonable and passes the spot check.

Note that (as I said before) circle rates are themselves a joke (from my personal conversations with property brokers, they’re 2-3 times lower than market rates currently in my area) and I’m taking the second lowest out of them for all, which is more than 15 times smaller than the highest.

World Bank’s estimate for total value of cropland and pastureland for 2018 was ~USD 2,070 per capita. This would make it about as large as our GDP in 2018 (current prices USD).

If we take this estimate into account, it would mean that non-agricultural land values are just about 1 times GDP. When compared with our land value estimate, it would mean that non-agricultural land is just 1 times GDP. I think this would be a gross underestimate given that non-agricultural activities contribute ~85% of our GDP, so non-agricultural land values would obviously be higher. For this reason, I think taking non-agricultural land value as 2 times GDP and so total land value as 3 times GDP would be better but I’ll provide rent figures in the rest of this post using both 2 and 3 times GDP.

2.3 Reasonable estimate for tax rates

The LVT rate can’t exceed the existing capitalization (cap) rates. I think it’s reasonable to assume a 2-3% cap rate for residential, 4-5% for industrial and 6-8% for commercial properties. Even though commercial and industrial land constitute a small percentage of the total land area, they’ll take a larger share of the land value pie. I think it’s reasonable to assume a weighted average cap rate of 4%.

Note that practically, during implementation, the government might tax properties at different rates based on different cap rates based on land-use and area, ideally based on up-to-date market surveys but we’re just taking a reasonable cap rate that can be multiplied with total land value to arrive at an estimate for the land rent tax base.

2.4 LVT revenue estimate

An 80% tax rate, to account for errors in land valuation, on land rents, i.e. an LVT of 80% of cap rate, would raise 80% x 4% x 2 x GDP, i.e. 6.4% of GDP. If land value was 3 times GDP, the same LVT would raise 9.6% of GDP.

3 Natural resources

3.1 Natural resource rents

Our natural resource rents are easily 2% of GDP in any given year (it’s been higher, in most years). At an 80% tax rate, we can easily raise 1.6% of GDP.

So I’ll just take the 80% of 2% of GDP, i.e. 1.6% of GDP figure for now.

Note that I have not included some revenue sources than may be counted as a land value tax, like a Vehicle Miles Travelled fee, Congestion charges, pollution tax, electromagnetic spectrum rent tax, etc—essentially, a tax on all kinds of rent from commons. I didn’t include these revenue sources because:

  • they are either small enough to not change the estimate by much, or

  • they’ll be only levied to offset certain expenditures (like the Vehicle Miles Travelled fee for the cost of building roads, etc), or

5 Potential

5.1 Replacing existing taxes

“Replacing existing taxes” can mean either reducing tax rates by that much or eliminating certain taxes altoghether while keeping others.

India’s tax collections were ~17.7% of GDP in FY23:

Total tax receipts for financial year 2022-23 were ₹48.4 trillion, comprising central tax revenue of ₹30.4 trillion (based on revised estimates) and states’ own tax revenue of ₹18 trillion (based on budget estimates). With a nominal GDP of ₹273 trillion, India’s tax-GDP ratio is 17.7%.

This means that an LVT can easily replace about 50% of the existing taxes even if we leave our natural resource rents (since we seem better at collecting them for legal mining and they’ll fall in the non-tax revenue for the government currently, i.e. outside the 17.7% figure). If we include natural resource rents, it can replace about 60% of the existing taxes.

5.2 Boosting government revenue/expenditure or eliminating the deficit

Alternatively, given that our government’s annual expenditure is ~28% of GDP, it can be used to either boost the government expenditure by more than a third of what it is currently, or completely elimite the deficit.

5.3 Citizen’s dividend

Dividing 10% of GDP by a population of 140 crore (1 crore = 10 million), we get around Rs. 20,000 per person per year. This would be around Rs. 1 lakh per family per year, assuming 5 people per family. And if we just distribute 10% of this revenue as dividends, we get around Rs. 2,000 per person per year, or around Rs. 10,000 per family per year.

Some in India want to downsize our military against China-Pakistan to half. This was also expected as the intention of the Agnipath scheme based on the initial information provided by the government; however, later statements painted a different picture. Regardless of whether this happens, it’s something that many find desirable. The usual argument that is put in favor of downsizing is that soldiers’ salaries and pensions are eating our budget at a time when we need to increase our capital expenditure (primarily buying more/upgrading weapons) and that there is a need to reduce the average age.

The table above shows that if we downsize our active and reserve personnel to half, we’ll be somewhere around (only slightly above) where the US is at currently. Now this is the level at which their (US) Army War College is suggesting partial conscription against Russia-China-Iran-DPRK because they suspect high casualties.

The Russia-Ukraine War is exposing significant vulnerabilities in the Army’s strategic personnel depth and ability to withstand and replace casualties.11 Army theater medical planners may anticipate a sustained rate of roughly 3,600 casualties per day, ranging from those killed in action to those wounded in action or suffering disease or other non-battle injuries.12 With a 25 percent predicted replacement rate, the personnel system will require 800 new personnel each day. For context, the United States sustained about 50,000 casualties in two decades of fighting in Iraq and Afghanistan. In large-scale combat operations, the United States could experience that same number of casualties in two weeks.13

In addition to the disciplined disobedience required to execute effective mission command, the US Army is facing a dire combination of a recruiting shortfall and a shrinking Individual Ready Reserve. This recruiting shortfall, nearly 50 percent in the combat arms career management fields, is a longitudinal problem. Every infantry and armor soldier we do not recruit today is a strategic mobilization asset we will not have in 2031.14 The Individual Ready Reserve, which stood at 700,000 in 1973 and 450,000 in 1994, now stands at 76,000.15 These numbers cannot fill the existing gaps in the active force, let alone any casualty replacement or expansion during a large-scale combat operation. The implication is that the 1970s concept of an all-volunteer force has outlived its shelf life and does not align with the current operating environment. The technological revolution described below suggests this force has reached obsolescence. Large-scale combat operations troop requirements may well require a reconceptualization of the 1970s and 1980s volunteer force and a move toward partial conscription.16

The casualty figures are from FM 4-0.

That doesn’t make our (post downsizing to half) number look good. However, there are several confounding factors here, the most important of which is the relative power between the countries, India and US, versus their respected non-China enemies (Pakistan for us while Russia, Iran and DPRK for them). The right way to go about this would be to get to a casualty number for us like the US did for them and then determine the optimal soldier. However, I’m not aware of any such estimation from the government.

2 Incompatibility with our military doctrine

I came across a rough estimation based on our “Cold Start” military doctrine (I’d suggest you read this paper to know about it in detail) when I posted this news (about the Army War College’s suggestion for a draft) on Reddit:

China can only sustain the combat consumption of 2 Group Armies in Tibet and another 2 Group Armies in Arunachal at maximum, each with 6 maneuver brigades. Assume a force coefficient of 1.5 in China’s favor, and that’s roughly 12 divisions that India must maintain vs China.

Vs Pakistan, India must maintain 20 divisions for credible defense. Up to 30 for a conventional offense.

India currently has 37 divisions, which is enough for a conventional offense vs Pakistan or a 2 front defensive war, but not both. This fits India’s current doctrine. If India downsizes, it will have to change its doctrine as well. […] I’m assuming a simple 1.0 force coef between India and Pakistan, and an assumption that 10 extra divisions can create a 3:1 force ratio along one corps-sized AOR, which then collapses the Pakistani front in that area and bisects the country as envisioned by Sundarji. Cold Start would have a different force requirement. Not thinking about nukes in this scenario or outside intervention.

This shows that downsizing to half is not compatible if we want to continue with the doctrine of Cold Start. Downsizing would mean that we would have to change our doctrine as well.

3 The poverty of the budget/capex argument

Even if it was compatible or if we have a better (or have changed) our doctrine, the entire budget argument seems bunkum to me. 50% of Rs. 1,18,889 crores for salaries and Rs. 1,19,300 crores for pensions for Army is about Rs. 1,19,094.5 crores. You’re telling me that getting rid of soldiers’ salaries and pensions is the best way to raise defense capital expenditure by a little north of Rs. 1 lakh crores out of all of Central govts non-defense spending of about Rs. 40 lakh crores or total government spending of about Rs. 80 lakh crores? That’s absurd!

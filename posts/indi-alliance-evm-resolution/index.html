Futility of machine-marked ballots

electronic voting
Author

Vinamr Sachdeva

Published

January 12, 2024

The opposition alliance came up with a resolution on the use of EVMs with VVPAT. It says:

Instead of the VVPAT slip falling in the box, it should be handed over to the voter who shall then place it in a separate ballot box after having verified his or her choice. 100% counting of VVPAT slips should then be done.

Hari Prasad et al on EVMs and Rahul Mehta’s “consecutive vote stealing” algorithm for VVPAT (and Meht’s demo on a VVPAT-look alike), which was also re-iterated by Hari Prasad, should have settled the discussion in favor of the side saying that EVMs, even with a VVPAT (with a black one-way glass), can be tampered (with malicious code) at the manufacturing site and then be signaled the winner at the time of First Level Checking (FLC) when they are connected with a symbol loading jig or PC/Laptop with a symbol loading application (yes! EVMs with VVPAT are not a standalone device), while also passing the mock poll test given that the clock and the stored vote counts can be used to start stealing after the mock poll and after a few real votes are cast. But we have these shenanigans.

What the opposition alliance has called for is similar to the ballot-marking devices (BMDs) used in some states in the US, usually for accessibility reasons (i.e., to make it usable for people with disabilities)—even though most all states use hand-marked paper ballots. With BMDs, the voter selects their choice(s) on a touchscreen-based machine, and then gets a printout of the ballot that they put in a ballot box. But Matt Bernhard and J. Alex Halderman of UMich found that 93% of voters did NOT recognize incorrectly printed ballots with such a setup! I think it’s like giving a Ctrl+P command for their voters—why is there going to be an error? So they don’t pay attention. This might be the reason why so many people in this public opinion video say something to the tune of “with an EVM, we don’t know where our vote goes.” I suspect even Indian voters might not be noticing the paper trail (note that they cannot even know, beyond a reasonable doubt, where their votes go even if they check the VVPAT for reasons stated earlier). And they might not notice it even if they got a machine-marked ballot in their hands like the UMich study showed for Americans. Even if 1/10th of the 93%, that didn’t notice incorrect machine-marked ballots in the study, didn’t notice them in a real election, it could change the result on many seats.

Another major fatal flaw in machine-marked ballots is the non-existence of any answer to the question: what should a voter do if they saw or received an incorrectly marked ballot? (This was mentioned by Mehta as well but I can’t find the original source.) If any government officer, or for that matter, any other person, saw their vote to confirm, it would violate ballot secrecy. Even if a “re- mock poll” is conducted again in case of a complaint by a voter, a tampered machine with a chip antenna and Bluetooth module (that can be used to signal when to steal), can be used to switch to honest working and then switched back to stealing when regular voting is taking place. If any voter had any issue with a hand-marked paper ballot, they would be given a new form to mark on and this could be repeated up to a point. But repeating has no value in a systematically tampered ballot marking machine.

(Sidenote: such a setup will also be vulnerable to an attack (courtesy: Mehta) where the VVPAT is tampered with by installing a custom-made printer than prints with a disappearing ink on the front side but with an “emerging” ink (visible with a time-delay) on the back side. But giving machine-marked ballots in the hands of voters is not enough even if we ignore this.)

Hand-marked paper ballots where voters are allowed to bring their own stamp or pen is the only way out with a secret ballot. However, I think we should also give voters an option to choose between an open non-secret ballot and a secret ballot.

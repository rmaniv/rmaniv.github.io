‘I guarantee you a 100% defeat’

history
war
Author

Vinamr Sachdeva

Published

March 21, 2021

In this thread, Shantanu Gupta, who is the author of a biography of the sitting Chief Minister of Uttar Pradesh, expounded upon his theory that Indira Gandhi, who was the Prime Minister at the time of the liberation of Bangladesh, mishandled the situation and that it was the Jan Sangh, which later merged into the Janta Party and was eventually succeeded by the Bhartiya Janta Party, which somehow compelled Indira Gandhi to act on the situation. His theory falls apart as soon as you take some basic facts into account. First, I think you should read his thread so that you get the timeline, without the “missing piece” which he ignores that will make his theory fall apart, right and so that this article doesn’t make you misunderstand Gupta’s position.

The missing piece to Gupta’s theory that completely misrepresents the situation at that time is very well explained by none other than the Chief of Army Staff at the time of that war, Sam Manekshaw, in an interview he gave later where he revisited his meeting with Indira Gandhi and her Cabinet on, I guess the 23rd of April, or around that time based on what he said in the interview. You can watch it starting at 5:10 from here and here’s a transcript of the relevant part that I wrote:

Mrs. Gandhi, in an awful temper, looked at me read out messages from the Chief Minister of West Bengal that thousands of refugees are pouring. From the Chief Minister of Assam, Chief Minister of Tripura. And she looked at me said, “What are you doing about it?” I said, “Nothing, what’s it going to do with me?” She said, “I want you to do something.” I said, “What do you want me to do?” She said, “I want you to march into East Pakistan.” I said, “That means war!” She said, “I don’t mind if it’s war.” I said, “Oh have you read the Bible? God said let there be light and there was light and you say let there be war and there’s war? Are you ready? I certainly am not. And I said, this was about 23rd of April, I said, ”You know, the Himalayan passes are opening and if the Chinese give us an ultimatum.” and I said, ”The monsoon will be breaking in a few days time and when it rains in that part of the world, it pours — rivers become like oceans, if you stand on one bank you can’t see the other, my movement will be confined to roads, because of climatic conditions the Air Force will not be able to support me, and if I were to go in, I guarantee you a hundred percent defeat. I said to Mr. Gandhi, Will you know give me your orders?” She said,”Alright, Cabinet would meet again at 4 o’clock.” Everybody started walking out, I being the junior one, smile on my face as I was walking out. She said, “Chief, stay behind.” I said, “Prime Minister, before you open your mouth, shall I send in my resignation on grounds of health, mental or physical? She said,”Oh, sit down Sam and tell me — everything you told me is the truth?” I said, “Yes, everything I’ve told you is the truth. It’s my job to fight. Fight to win, not to lose.” She smiled at me and said, “Alright Sam, you let me know when you’re ready.”

(emphasis mine)

Now this was around the April of 1971, when the Chief of Army Staff tells the Prime Minister that he can’t win a war at that time because during the monsoon season, there’s heavy rainfall in the region where there’s East Pakistan (now Bangladesh), West Bengal, Assam. And in that heavy rainfall, the Army wouldn’t be able to travel off-roads, the Air Force wouldn’t be able to support them and that he guarantees her a 100% defeat. And Gupta, in his thread criticizes Indira Gandhi for not intervening in May and July! Essentially, when we would have been defeated due to climatic conditions according to that Chief of Army Staff at that time.

One can point out other flaws in Indira Gandhi’s handling of the situation and maybe pressure from opposition parties was actually a contributory factor in her decision to intervene but as far as not acting at the time of monsoon is concerned, there’s no case to be made against her.
